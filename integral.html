integrals

Integrals are the opposite of derivatives. They help you find the total amount of something, like area under a curve. If derivatives tell you how fast something is changing, integrals tell you how much has built up over time. You‚Äôll use them to calculate things like distance, area, and accumulation. Think of an integral as adding up tiny pieces to find a whole.

Indefinite

Derivatives answer the question:

"Given a function, what's the rate of change (slope) at any point?"

So if you have a function like f(x) = x2, then its derivative is
f′(x) = 2x. That means the slope of the curve x2 at any point x is 2x.

Indefinite integrals do the opposite of derivatives. They answer:

"Given the slope (derivative), what was the original function?"

So if you're told:

"The derivative is 2ùë•; what function did that come from?"

You'd "undo" the derivative and get:

∫2xdx= x2+C

That "+ C" is called the constant of integration. Why? Because if the original function was x2 + 5 or x2 - 10, the derivative would still be 2x.

So we don't know the exact starting function‚Äîjust its general family. That's why we call it an indefinite integral.

This is the formula:

∫ f(x) dx = F(x) +C , where F′(x)=f(x)

Power Rule

If you have a function f(x) = xn, where n ≠ -1, then:

∫xndx= xn+1 n+1 +C

This is essentially the reverse of the power rule for derivatives.

Why it works?
You already know that:

d dx ( xn+1 n+1 )=xn

So, integrating xn should give you that original function back (plus the constant of integration C).

This is because the derivative of ln|x| is 1/x, not anything involving powers of x.

Definite

What is a Definite Integral?

While an indefinite integral gives you a general function plus a constant...

A definite integral gives you a specific number‚Äîthe net area under the curve from one point to another:

∫ a b f (x) dx

  • f(x) is your function (like x2, sin(x), etc.)
  • a is the lower limit of integration
  • b is the upper limit of integration
  • dx tells you what you are integrating in respect to (x, y, etc.)

How to Solve

Step 1: Find the indefinite integral (the antiderivative):

∫f(x)dx=F(x)+C

...but since you're doing a definite integral, you don't include the + C because it cancels out in the next step.

Step 2: Plug in the upper and lower limits:

∫ a b f (x) dx = F (b) − F (a)

This is called evaluating the integral.

Net Change

The Net Change Theorem uses a definite integral to calculate how much a quantity has increased or decreased over time.

This is the formula:

If a quantity Q(t) changes over time and its rate of change is given by Q‚Ä≤(t), then:

∫abQ‚Ä≤(t)dt=Q(b)−Q(a)

Integrating the rate of change gives you how much total change happened between t = a and t = b

Total Change

The Total Change Theorem gives you the total amount accumulated (like distance or total fluid), regardless of direction.

This is the formula:

If Q(t) is a rate of change, and you're interested in the total accumulated amount (no cancelling from positive and negative values,) use:

Total Change = ∫ab|Q‚Ä≤(t)|dt

Key Difference:

  • Net change allows positives and negatives to cancel out
  • Total change takes the absolute value of the rate so that all change counts positively.
