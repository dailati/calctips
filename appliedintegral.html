areas & volumes

Applied integrals use integration to find areas and volumes in geometric contexts. You can find the area between two curves by subtracting the lower function from the upper one. For volumes, you use methods like the disk and washer methods, which revolve a region around an axis to create a solid. Volume by cross sections involves stacking known shapes along a curve. These techniques help turn 2D graphs into meaningful 3D measurements.

Area Between Two Curves

When you have two functions, one on top of the other, the area between their graphs over an interval can be found using integration.

This method finds the net vertical distance between the two curves and adds it up across an interval.

General Formula (Vertical Slices):

If f(x) ≥ g(x) on the interval [a, b], then the area between the curves is:

Area = ∫ a b [f(x)- g(x)]dx

  • f(x): top function
  • g(x): bottom function
  • [a,b]: interval on the x-axis
  • At each point, f(x) - g(x) gives the vertical gap between the curves.
  • Integration adds up all those little slices from left to right.

What It Represents:

Horizontal Slices (in terms fo y):

If the functions are described as x = f(y) and x = g(y), and you're integrating horizontally, then:

Area = ∫ c d [f(y)- g(y)]dy

  • f(y): rightmost function
  • g(y): leftmost function
  • [c,d]: interval on the y-axis

When to Use Each:

  • Use vertical slices if curves are functions of x (standard case).
  • Use horizontal slices if curves are easier to express as functions of y or if they switch top/bottom but not left/right.

Disk Method / Washer Method

The disk and washer methods are techniques used in integral calculus to find the volume of a solid of revolution——a 3D shape formed by rotating a region around an axis. These methods take advantage of slicing the solid into thin circular cross-sections. The disk method applies when the solid has no hole in the middle, using solid disks as slices. The washer method is used when there’s a hole, and the slices look like washers (disks with holes). Both methods involve integrating the area of these circular slices across the interval of rotation.

The Washer Method

The washer method is used to find the volume of a solid of revolution when the region being revolved does not touch the axis of revolution. This creates a solid with a "hole" in the middle, resembling a washer.

General Formula (Rotation about the x-axis):

If the outer radius is R(x) and the inner radius is r(x) on the interval [a,b], the volume is:

Volume =π ∫ a b ( [R(x)2- [r(x)]2 ) dx

  • R(x): distance from the axis of revolution to the outer boundary of the region.
  • r(x): distance from the axis of revolution to the inner boundary of the region.
  • [a,b]: interval on the x-axis

What It Represent:

  • At each x, π[R(x)]2 is the area of the outer disk.
  • At each x, π[r(x)]2 is the area of the inner "hole".
  • The difference gives the area of the washer at that x
  • Integration adds up the volumes of these infinitesimally thin washers.

Rotation about the y-axis:

If the outer radius is R(y) and the inner radius is r(y) on the interval [c,d], the volume is:

Volume =π ∫ c d ( [R(y)2- [r(y)]2 ) dy

  • R(y): distance from the axis of revolution to the outer boundary (as a function of y).
  • r(y): distance from the axis of revolution to the inner boundary (as a function of y).
  • [c,d]: interval on the y-axis

When to Use:

Use the washer method when the region being revolved has a gap between it and the axis of revolution.

The Disk Method

The disk method is a special case of the washer method used when the region being revolved is flush against the axis of revolution. The resulting solid is "solid" with no hole through the middle.

General Formula (Rotation about the x-axis):

If the radius of the disk at x is R(x) on the interval [a,b], the volume is:

Volume =π ∫ a b ( R(x)2 ) dx

  • R(x): distance from the axis of revolution to the boundary of the region.
  • [a,b]: interval on the x-axis

What It Represents:

  • At each x, π[R(x)]2 is the area of a circular disk.
  • Integration adds up the volumes of these infinitesimally thin disks.

Rotation about the y-axis:

If the radius of the disk at y is R(y) on the interval [c,d], the volume is:

Volume =π ∫ c d ( R(y)2 ) dy

  • R(y): distance from the axis of revolution to the boundary (as a function of y).
  • [c,d]: interval on the y-axis

When to Use:

Use the disk method when the region being revolved is directly adjacent to the axis of revolution. In this case, the "inner radius" of the washer method becomes zero.

Connection:

Notice that if r(x) = 0 in the washer method formula, you get the disk method formula. The disk method is essentially the washer method where the inner radius is zero.

Volume By Cross-Sections

This method calculates the volume of a 3D solid by knowing the area of its cross-sections perpendicular to a given axis (either the x or y-axis). Imagine slicing the solid into many thin "slabs." If you know the area of each slab's face and its thickness, you can find the total volume by integrating these areas over the length of the solid.

General Formula (Cross-sections perpendicular to the x-axis):

If the area of the cross-section at a given x is A(x), and the solid extends from x=a to x=b, then the volume V is:

Volume = ∫ a b A(x) dx

  • A(x): the area of the cross-section at a specific x-value.
  • dx: the infinitesimal thickness of the cross-section.
  • [a,b]: the interval on the x-axis over which the solid extends.

General Formula (Cross-sections perpendicular to the y-axis):

If the area of the cross-section at a given y is A(y), and the solid extends from y=c to y=d, then the volume V is:

Volume = ∫ c d A(y) dy

  • A(y): the area of the cross-section at a specific y-value.
  • dy: the infinitesimal thickness of the cross-section.
  • [c,d]: the interval on the y-axis over which the solid extends.

Steps to Find the Volume:

  1. Visualize the Solid and the Base: Understand the region that forms the base of the solid.
  2. Determine the Orientation of Cross-Sections: Are they perpendicular to the x-axis or the y-axis? This will dictate whether you integrate with respect to x or y.
  3. Find the Area of a General Cross-Section, A(x) or A(y): This is the crucial step. The shape of the cross-section (square, triangle, semicircle, etc.) will determine how you calculate its area. The dimensions of this shape will be related to the boundaries of the base region.
  4. Determine the Limits of Integration: Find the range of x or y values over which the solid extends.
  5. Set Up and Evaluate the Integral: Use the appropriate formula and integrate A(x) or A(y) over the determined limits.

Example:

Consider a solid whose base is the region bounded by y=√x, the x-axis, and x=4. The cross-sections perpendicular to the x-axis are squares. Find the volume of this solid.

  1. Base: Region under y=√x from x=0 to x=4.
  2. Orientation: Perpendicular to the x-axis, so we'll integrate with respect to x.
  3. Area A(x): The side of each square cross-section extends from the x-axis to the curve y=√x. So, the side length of the square is s=√x. The area of the square is A(x)=s2=(√x)2=x.
  4. Limits: The solid extends from x=0 to x=4.
  5. Integral:

    Volume = ∫ 0 4 A(x) dx = ∫ 0 4 x dx

  6. Evaluating the integral:

    Volume = [x2 2 ] 0 4 = ( 42 2 ) -( 02 2) = 16 2 = 8

The volume of the solid is 8 cubic units.

Shell Method

The shell method is another technique to find the volume of a solid of revolution. Instead of slicing the solid perpendicular to the axis of revolution (like in the disk/washer method), here we slice parallel to the axis of revolution, creating thin cylindrical "shells."

General Formula (Rotation about the y-axis):

If a region is bounded by y=f(x), the x-axis, and the lines x=a and x=b (where a≥0), and it's revolved about the y-axis, the volume is:

Volume =2π ∫ a b x⋅f(x) dx

  • x: the radius of the cylindrical shell (distance from the y-axis).
  • f(x): the height of the cylindrical shell.
  • dx: the infinitesimal thickness of the shell.
  • [a,b]: the interval on the x-axis.

General Formula (Rotation about the x-axis):

If a region is bounded by x=g(y), the y-axis, and the lines y=c and y=d (where c≥0), and it's revolved about the x-axis, the volume is:

Volume =2π ∫ c d y⋅g(y) dy

  • y: the radius of the cylindrical shell (distance from the x-axis).
  • g(y): the height of the cylindrical shell.
  • dy: the infinitesimal thickness of the shell.
  • [c,d]: the interval on the x-axis.

What It Represents:

  • Imagine a thin vertical strip at a particular x. When revolved about the y-axis, it forms a cylindrical shell with radius approximately x, height approximately f(x), and a small thickness dx.
  • The surface area of this cylindrical shell (if laid flat) would be approximately 2πx⋅f(x).
  • Multiplying by the thickness dx gives the approximate volume of that shell: 2πx⋅f(x)⋅dx.
  • Integration sums the volumes of these infinitesimally thin shells across the interval [a,b].

The logic is similar for rotation about the x-axis, just with the roles of x and y (and f and g) swapped appropriately.

When to Use:

The shell method is particularly useful when:

  • The axis of revolution is parallel to the slicing direction.
  • The function is easier to integrate with respect to the variable that is parallel to the axis of revolution.
  • The disk/washer method would require solving for the inverse function, which might be difficult.

Example:

Find the volume of the solid generated by revolving the region bounded by y=x2, y=0, and x=1 about the y-axis.

  • Identify the function and interval: f(x)=x2, interval [0,1].
  • Axis of revolution: y-axis.
  • Set up the integral (using the formula for rotation about the y-axis):

    • Volume =2π ∫ 0 1 x⋅x2 dx =2π ∫ 0 1 x3 dx

  • Evaluate the integral:

    • Volume =2π [x4 4 ] 0 1 dx =2π [14 4 ] -2π [04 4 ] = π 2

The volume of the solid is=π2.
